## Phoneme Candidate Generation (Phase B2 Preview)

Based on distributional evidence, the following phonemes are candidates:

### Hypothesis 1: Pharyngeal Consonant (Semitic)

**Candidate**: /ʕ/ (voiced pharyngeal fricative) or /ħ/ (voiceless pharyngeal fricative)

**Evidence FOR**:
- Strong word-initial position (typical of Semitic pharyngeals)
- Absent in Greek phonology (explains dropping in Linear B)
- 82% at HT (administrative center with Semitic vocabulary)
- Phonotactic constraint matches Akkadian/West Semitic patterns

**Evidence AGAINST**:
- Would expect more ligatures if representing common syllable
- Standalone use with numerals unusual for syllabogram

**Score**: MEDIUM

### Hypothesis 2: Palatalized Consonant (Luwian)

**Candidate**: /kʲ/ (palatalized velar) or /ɲ/ (palatal nasal)

**Evidence FOR**:
- Luwian has rich palatalized consonant inventory
- -WA-JA suffix strongly associated with *301 (Luwian particles)
- Position constraints match Luwian phonotactics

**Evidence AGAINST**:
- Would expect more medial occurrences
- Palatals usually distribute more evenly in words

**Score**: MEDIUM

### Hypothesis 3: Logogram/Abbreviation

**Candidate**: Not a phonetic sign but a logogram (ideogram)

**Evidence FOR**:
- 82.6% standalone + numeral pattern
- Functions like commodity logograms (VIN, OLE, etc.)
- Limited syllabic combinations

**Evidence AGAINST**:
- Appears syllabically in A-TA-I-*301-WA-JA
- Variant forms (*301-WA, *301-NA) suggest phonetic value

**Score**: LOW (for fully logographic interpretation)

### Hypothesis 4: Hybrid Sign

**Candidate**: Primarily logographic with secondary phonetic value

**Evidence FOR**:
- Dual distribution (administrative standalone + religious syllabic)
- Similar to Linear B where some signs have logographic AND phonetic uses
- Explains both patterns

**Score**: HIGH
